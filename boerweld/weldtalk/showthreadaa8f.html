    I don't know how old you are, but something like oil rig welding is what I could call a young man's game. I'm 30, and at this stage in my life, I would call myself too old to get started in that.

    Be prepared to get very dirty and blow black stuff out of your nose every day. If that doesn't sound apealing to you, you may be best to stay with what you are doing now. It was not apealing to myself, and that's one reason why I went back to fixing computers for a living. I come home alot cleaner with more money then what I made welding.

    The other two posters have given you good advice if you are seroius about this... a trip to a welding school would be well advised as well.

    i will be blunt, and tell you i do not recommend schooling in your instance as others have. i have hired and fired plenty, and can tell you that guys that learn from someone that knows what they are doing, are much more trainable and versatile. i have had guys under me that have been welding for 30 years, taught themselves everything they know. The guys that have been welding for 2 years and learned from a true expert can nearly always blow the homegrown farmhand welder out of the water.
    get a 250 amp or larger mig welder, miller or lincoln, but preferably miller(oops, maybe I shouldn't be so partial ). As you learn at your side job, take that knowledge home and practice it.

    weldaway, I ask this question with the utmost respect to your position as someone who is in a place to hire/fire and knows what works.

    Why is it you don't recommend welding school? I'd be intersted to hear you expound on your statement. Is it becuase you have found that people going thru school are not as open minded to your way of doing things? I'm also curious what your business specializes in.

    If you are willing to hire a newbie that you like, for whatever reason, with no expeince in welding at all, and train him, my hat is truly off to you. I wish there were more employers with that attitude... the world would be a better place I think. You can find all the experinced people in the world, but finding workers with good "employability skills" can be very difficult. Showing up for work on time, prepared to get busy, and ask questions, is often more important then how much expereince you have.

    I fear, however, not all employers have that attitude, and sometimes you need the "sheepskin" just to get your foot in the door.
