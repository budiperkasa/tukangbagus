    I've been welding over 30 years and that is a picture of a halfway decent flat weld with at least 3 passes, not a root on a vert. You need to watch your speed on your passes to make them more consistent, notice the long lobes on some of your ripples (>>>>), and try to make your passes straighter. Dings from a chipping hammer are considered to be stress raisers and are regarded the same as undercut or arc strikes. They also don't look very good on an x-ray either.

    Remember, good enough seldom is. Each weld is your signature, so make each one your very best!

    You need to work on your communication skills. Being able to give a coherent turnover to the next shift is a very important skill in the welding trade. The days of the illiterate welder are long gone!

    You seem to have determination and are doing pretty well skill wise. Work hard at getting to be the best and never give up.

    Good luck, Bob.

    With a 1/8" root opening you should have one pass to tie both plates together. The reason I believe it was welded in the 1G position instead of 3G is the flatness of the weld and the complete lack of undercut (which is good!).

    It is the norm to have a slight convex shape to a vertical stringer with 7018 and not the very flat profile that you have achieved. I've known some extremely talented welders in my years and I don't think any of them could get a flat multiple pass weld like that in 3G. The law of gravity is working against you. All would have a slightly rounded surface on a full width bead.

    If, however, you can do that in a 3G as a student you are in for one heck of a career as a welder. Good luck, Bob.
