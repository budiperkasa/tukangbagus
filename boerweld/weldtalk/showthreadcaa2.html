    As I Suspected

    I suspected from the beginning that one could weld using the cutting torch, since the only difference in the two is size of the opening in the tip. The smaller opening in the cutting tip and the higher operating voltage (210 to 220) is to generate greater flame velocity so as to blow the metal out of the cut. I was attaching a slide bolt to the trailer hitch to lock it onto the ball and decided to see what I could do with the cutting torch. I set the voltage at 150 (170 is recommended for normal welding) and using only water on Mode 2 setting 2 I was able to produce these two welds. The metal in the slide bolt is very light and you have to be quick but the Plasma flame it needle sharp and I'm anxious to see what I can do using some plain steel wire and perhaps experiment using the water alcohol mixture. I'll probably just change over the tip in the welding torch rather than to drain and replace the water in the cutting torch. Since I am dealing with a true plasma rather than either a combustion or arc generated flame there is probably not the range of temperature controls. The machine is advertised as producing a plasma stream ranging from 8,000 to 14,800 degrees F with its lowest still being hotter than OA which burns at about 3500 degrees F.

    This is a different animal and as such requires a different diet. I did not try to go back over the welds but left them just as they were for reference purposes.

    Hi Flatty, in most cases you'll find butt welding is the easiest joint to achieve.

    With a lap joint you need to weld both sides of the overlap.

    In a butt joint type weld, the weld pool will cause a sunken joint without some filler, but if you let the weld pool width get to 2/3rds the thickness of the steel in width, that's a good indication of how deep the weld pool is.

    So if you have 1/4" thick steel and you butt the ends of the joint together, when the steel melts you judge it when the weld pool gets to about 3/16" wide....if you let the pool get bigger it'll melt through and you get an awful runaway with a hole.

    I found also if you lay a 3mm welding rod (with the flux coating knocked off) on the joint and then proceed to weld along the seam the welding rod will automatically add enough filler to make a nice level weld seam as you work along it.

    As you say, it's a piece of cake going over a previously welded seam without having to grind out any metal.

    One piece of advice.......anything that has zinc on it will play the devil with your welds........I grind the stuff off wherever I have to weld on it.

    Fencing wire is not the best filler rod due to the possible zinc coating, but can suffice if you have only lots of that material.

    I've also found the thicker the filler rod (3/8" is good) the longer it lasts.

    If you really must, as in the material at hand, weld tubing that has a zinc coating, like water pipe or square steel tubing etc, hit the weld zone with the heat first by holding the torch away a bit, and when the zinc bubbles and goes whitish give it a vigorous brushing.......it'll get rid of most of the zinc.......but avoid the fumes at all costs.

    Sometimes where you have a panel that is completely coated with zinc, you can best join the material by brazing ( Mode 1) with copper or brass wire.........copper wire twisted together works well as a brazing medium.
    Have fun......give aluminium a go, but use powdered flux on both filler rod and joint, heating the rod first to coat it.
    Ian.

    Finding Wire:

    It appears that finding bare untreated wire larger than 1/16 inch is not easy. I can get small rolls of what we call "stove" wire at the local lumber and hardware stores but most other wire available locally is lightly galvanized. Knocking the flux off of welding rod on a regular basis kind of defeats the purpose. Back in my days around the ship yards I could have gotten all the discarded welding rod I could have hauled, because once they had removed the days intended supply from the hot boxes, what ever was left over was discarded. The light zinc coating under the heat of the plasma flame quickly dissipates, but the slight amount of fumes are a bit of a bother. I will have to go over the area around each weld with a wire brush before painting to remove the zinc oxide haze.

    I've got to get to work now on wiring and setting up the new portion of the shop so I will probably not be doing much welding for a week or two.     Hi, I quite agree, the source of suitable filler is where you find it.

    Some years ago I went to an auction of miscellaneous tools and stuff and got 4 boxes of 3.2mm welding rods for a dollar a box simply because nobody bid on them.....boxes had been opened and a few rods used, that's why I've been knocking the flux off the rods as I need them.

    They're a bit thin for my liking, as I find that 9mm diam steel rod works best for me, like the stuff you find on building sites after the re-bar has been laid and offcuts get dumped.....it's all good steel, and being thicker than welding rods lasts longer.

    If you can get the single offcut lengths of re-bar with a few cross pieces still attached you can hold the rod in the vice and give the cross pieces a twist and they practically fall off, being only spot welded where they cross each other, other than that a big bolt cutter soon reduces rebar lengths to single pieces.

    You're lucky you've got the good weather for outside working at the moment......down in OZ the outside temps down South Melbourne way are 10 and 12 deg C during the day, cold by our standards, and makes working outside or in the garage (unheated) unless rugged up, a chore.

    One of these days I'm going to make a solar heater to go on the garage roof and feed warm air inside to get the temp up a bit.......it's insulated with Polystyrene foam boards under the steel roof, and it tends to keep the inside temperature down at the overnight low, and all day long too.

    I see some people have made them using aluminium Coke cans painted black and laid in a sealed box with a plastic cover.......might give it a go.......the wood heater you mentioned sounds interesting.......could be another project.............(I need more irons in the fire)....LOL.

    I toyed with the idea of using my rain water storage tank, situated outside at the back of the garage.....5,000 litres.....as a solar storage device.......storing the water at 25 deg C and just continuously pumping the water (solar pond pump) to a fan blown radiator in the garage.....that would get the inside up to near 20 deg C which is almost normal as the insulation would keep it pretty constant.

    At this time of the year, with lots of rain, the tank is not used, and in Summer when it is, the heater would be turned off.

    Have fun with your carpentry....

    The Zig Zag Curve continues:

    I had said that i would not be doing any more welding until I had finished with the shop. I Lied. As is most often the case in my life one task only leads to another and I needed to pour a little concrete but moving the cement mixer around by hand was getting to be a little more than I cared to do, so I stopped to build a tow bar for my cement mixer so that I could just tow it around when and where I needed it with the lawn tractor. The main bar was constructed out of heavy wall 1 inch square tubing but needed to use some old hay rake tines for the tow loop and attach a slide bolt to lock the loop around the ball.

    If there is an obvious fault with the Multiplaz it might be that it is too hot for working with some thinner metals. It is fine when working with mixed gauges as you can concentrate the heat on the heavier piece and sneak up on the lighter gauge so to speak, but you do have to be quick. It appears that the minimum plasma temperature in mode 2 when the flame stream is concentrated between the torch and the work piece is still around 8,000 F though I have no way to measure this. I had on a previous test tried welding with the smaller cutting tip to reduce the temp and while this does improve conditions the end result is that the flame is only smaller not cooler or at least at this point that is how it appears to me. It may be that smaller is not the answer but rather larger and by spreading the heat over a larger surface the effective working temperature might be lower in the area of interest. I'll need to play with this later and report back on my findings.

    Hi, what you say is quite true......I found that for welding, Mode 2 is the only method.....Mode 1 just puts out a large flame spread and the whole shebang gets to red heat.

    I've made quite a few square tube projects, mostly with 20mm X 1.5mm wall stuff as I have/had a lot of 1 meter offcuts left over from a project.

    With tube like this I found you need to get in close to enable the lightening bolt in Mode 2 to form, then when the metal melts you draw away a bit to break the spark and do this working round the joint progressively.

    Too much Mode 2 in one spot continuously will create a hole if you're not careful.

    Otto Nobedder on Welding Tips and Tricks suggested that you can use the cutting nozzle and no alcohol in the water with the lowest setting in Mode 2 for more welding control when you do the small stuff.

    One thing I'm contemplating and that is to get a small petrol generator, selling on EBAY for $400, rated at 4KVA, which as the description states, will give you 3,500 watts peak and 3,000 watts continuous......two 240 volt 15 amp outlets and a 12 volt 8 amp one too.......mainly as an emergency power source for my deep freezers in case we have a power loss, but also as I want to have a portable welding outfit for all occasions etc.

    It will be my "have welder will travel" mode for jobs down the bottom of the garden where the Gnomes and Elves hang out....LOL

    It's coming into spring time down in OZ and at the moment I'm lopping the tops off dead tree limbs as we've had some heavy wind lately and my big Cootamundra Wattle tree, which is 33years old and full of worm holes, broke a large piece of the upper trunk off, and it weighs a ton, very hard wood.

    Last year I invested in one of those grass trimmers with all the attachments, like a hedge trimmer, brush cutter and a long pole chain saw, driven by a 4 stroke Honda C25 engine.

    So the guy at the yard said it was good for high branches, max 25mm diam etc......oh yeah........well the broken bit on the tree was 150mm diam, or 6" if you prefer Imperial.........going from both sides it cut through the 6" like butter, and all that 5 metres away up in the air.
