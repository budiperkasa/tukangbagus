    Hi Jenny, apologies for not getting this out sooner.

    The first link <http://cmcmmi.com/products-services/stainless-steel-rebar/> has the following:

    "Generally, all stainless steel rebar, regardless of chemistry, is produced to a minimum yield of 75ksi and minimum ultimate tensile of 100ksi. The austenitic grades of stainless steel (316L and XM-28), particularly in larger diameter bars, may however be available only in Grade 60. For design applications requiring GR75 reinforcing steel, the duplex chemistries of 2304 and 2205 should only be specified.

    The ASTM A955/A955M minimum elongation of an 8” specimen for stainless steel rebar is 20% making stainless considerably more ductile than even ASTM A706/706M low alloy steel. Because of the high yield and tensile strengths of 2304 & 2205 duplex grades of stainless steel rebar, their elongation may test at less than 20% but greater than that of the 12% elongation of A706 carbon rebar.

    The exceptional ductility of stainless steel rebar makes it an excellent choice for seismic applications."
    ================================================== =======================

    Back when I worked construction, there were two grades of carbon steel rebar generally used, Grade 40 and Grade 60; the numbers correspond to their tensile strength with G60 obviously being stronger. The Grade 60 was almost always special order, these days it is easier to find in stock at the yards. It is the highest rated that I have found near me.

    The citation above states that stainless rebar is typically Grade 75 (first paragraph). So the specification recognizes the increased rating for tensile strength.

    The third paragraph points out the advantage of using it in seismic zones.

    Here on the U.S. East Coast we (normally!) don't get earthquakes so where I live is not considered a seismic zone. The rebar I see going into bridges is a vinyl or plastic-coated type that resists corrosion better but to my knowledge is still carbon steel. The road crews go crazy with salt whenever snow threatens and the green plastic coating protects the steel. Stainless steel does not need a secondary coating for corrosion protection.
